\section{Results and Analysis}

\subsection{Full-Document vs. Citation-Context Queries}

Table~\ref{tab:query-ablation} compares two query views on COLIEE 2026 validation: full-document queries and citation-context queries. Both models show the same conclusion: the full document is clearly better. For SAILER, F1@5 drops from 0.1213 to 0.1022; for ModernBERT DAPT+SFT, it drops from 0.1650 to 0.1038. The same downward trend in SAILER matters because it shows that this is not unique to ModernBERT. At the same time, the ablation is conservative rather than perfectly length-matched: the citation-context view is much shorter on average, but it is not hard-capped at 512 words; 512 English word tokens only serves as the threshold for fallback expansion.

\subsection{Dynamic Negatives and Year Scope Constraints}

Following a THUIR-style setup, we first used BM25 top-100 candidates as a hard-negative pool and sampled 15 negatives per update. On COLIEE 2025 validation, however, this gives only about 0.00515 F1, nearly identical to random guessing at 0.00451. This shows that negative difficulty in legal case retrieval cannot be approximated by lexical rank alone. Once we replace the static design with dynamic negative sampling based on current model scores, the exploratory 2025 validation F1 jumps to 0.12834.

Scope filtering adds a second layer of stability. Its legal intuition is straightforward: a judge may cite prior cases, but not future ones. On COLIEE 2025 validation, simply adding year scope constraints to 4096-token ModernBERT fp16 SFT raises F1@5 from 0.11931 to 0.12189. The gain is modest but consistent, and it matches the time order of legal citations.

A post-hoc audit further shows that the early train/validation shared scope was built from cleaned full-document text rather than from raw judgments. This was not ideal, but the discrepancy is limited: for the 2,001 train/validation queries in COLIEE 2026, the global Jaccard overlap between full-document-text-based and raw-based scope is 0.9447, the mean per-query Jaccard is 0.9291, and the full-document-text-based scope still covers 97.86\% of the raw-based candidates on average. The legacy test scope is also highly similar to the raw test scope, and the final official submission switches back to raw-year scope.

Another implementation detail must be stated explicitly: the current scope does not delete gold positives. It restricts only similarity scoring and the negative candidate pool, while positives still come directly from the official labels. An audit of the current contrastive JSON files shows that 256/6,595 (3.88\%) training positives and 81/1,654 (4.90\%) validation positives still fall outside the heuristic year scope but remain in training on purpose. This is intentional, because the year extractor is still rule-based; using the scope to delete positives would risk removing true noticed cases.

\subsection{Learning to Rank and Post-processing}

Table~\ref{tab:ltr-features} lists the exact 28 features used in the current LTR pipeline, covering seven families of signals: lexical, dense, rank position, length, placeholder, year, and chunk aggregation. \texttt{query\_id} and \texttt{candidate\_id} are identifiers only and are not used as training features.

To judge whether LTR really helps, it is not enough to compare the official test runs \texttt{task1\_flnlpembed} and \texttt{task1\_flnlpltr}, because those runs use different cut-off settings. To make dense-only and LTR directly comparable, all \texttt{nDCG@10} values in this table are recomputed from the full 2026 validation labels. The result is consistent: LTR alone does not improve raw top-5 F1, but it raises raw \texttt{nDCG@10} from 0.253970 to 0.258978; after each side applies its best cut-off, the LTR pipeline improves F1 from 0.172535 to 0.178258 and \texttt{nDCG@10} from 0.222777 to 0.231970. In short, the gain from LTR is modest but real: it improves the quality of the top-ranked list, which later helps cut-off search recover a better final set.

Finally, comparing our results with Table~\ref{tab:thuir-table3-excerpt} makes the long-context argument clearer. In the THUIR excerpt, unlimited BM25 reaches F1 of 0.1541, while 512-token SAILER reaches 0.1385, so the lexical model is higher by 0.0156. On our held-out COLIEE 2026 validation split, by contrast, BM25 reaches F1 of 0.1499 and ModernBERT + DAPT + SFT reaches 0.1650, exceeding BM25 by 0.0151; on the same split, SAILER reaches only 0.1213, which is 0.0286 below BM25. These numbers strongly support our main claim: for legal case retrieval, a long-context encoder that can read 4096 tokens is already clearly stronger than BERT-like models limited to 512 tokens. Even though our current hardware fully covers only 56.11\% of the judgments at 4096 tokens, the long-context model still surpasses an unlimited-length lexical baseline.
