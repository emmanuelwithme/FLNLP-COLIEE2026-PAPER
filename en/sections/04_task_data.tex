\section{Task and Data}

We use COLIEE 2026 Task~1 as the main benchmark and COLIEE 2025 as auxiliary data for methodological exploration and failure analysis. Task~1 is defined as follows: given a query case, retrieve the most relevant prior cases from a candidate case collection and evaluate the ranked output with top-$k$ metrics.

\subsection{Data Sources and Splits}

In our current setup, the COLIEE 2026 case collection contains 7,708 documents. We further split the official labels into 1,600 training queries and 401 validation queries. Model training and hyperparameter selection are performed on this train/validation split. Our main analysis reports top-5 micro-averaged F1, precision, and recall on both the 2026 validation and 2026 training queries. COLIEE 2025 is mainly used to document the failure of early training strategies, the effect of dynamic negative sampling, and the early gain from scope filtering.

\subsection{\texttt{processed} and \texttt{processed\_new}}

We compare two query representations. The first is \texttt{processed}, which uses the full preprocessed case text as both query and candidate. The second is \texttt{processed\_new}, which follows the reference sentence extraction idea from THUIR: it keeps only local context around \texttt{FRAGMENT\_SUPPRESSED}, \texttt{REFERENCE\_SUPPRESSED}, and \texttt{CITATION\_SUPPRESSED}, and then pads the result with preprocessed text when it is too short. For fair ablation, candidates are always encoded from \texttt{processed}; only the query representation is switched.

The \texttt{processed} corpus is generated by \texttt{process.py}. The script starts from the first body paragraph marker \texttt{[1]} in each raw judgment, removes leading blank and fragment-only lines, merges line-broken text back into sentence-like units, strips surface suppression placeholders, editorial markers such as \texttt{[translation]}, \texttt{[sic]}, and \texttt{[DATE\_SUPPRESSED]}, and filters out French content by language detection. When the body does not already contain a summary, \texttt{summary.py} may prepend an extracted summary from the raw file. The result is a cleaned full-document representation that keeps the legal content while removing obvious noise.

The \texttt{processed\_new} corpus is generated by \texttt{reference.py}. It first sentence-tokenizes the raw judgment and extracts only sentences containing \texttt{FRAGMENT\_SUPPRESSED}, \texttt{REFERENCE\_SUPPRESSED}, or \texttt{CITATION\_SUPPRESSED}, together with one adjacent context sentence: if the placeholder occurs in the first half of a sentence, the previous sentence is kept; otherwise the next sentence is kept. The extracted text then goes through nearly the same cleanup and language filtering steps as \texttt{processed}. If the result is still shorter than 512 English word tokens, the script prepends leading sentences from the \texttt{processed} version until it approaches the 512-word threshold. In other words, \texttt{processed\_new} is not a pure citation fragment; it is a reference-centered query representation with fallback expansion from the full cleaned document.

\subsection{Long-Document Characteristics}

As shown in Table~\ref{tab:length-stats}, COLIEE 2026 \texttt{processed} documents are clearly long. More importantly, 94.80\% of them are shorter than 12,288 tokens. This justifies our later learning-to-rank design with three 4096-token chunks: most cases can be covered almost completely by chunk-based reranking without increasing the encoder context limit.

\subsection{Domain-Adaptive Pretraining Corpus}

Besides the official COLIEE data, we use the Hugging Face dataset \texttt{common-pile/caselaw\_access\_project\_filtered} for continued pretraining: \url{https://huggingface.co/datasets/common-pile/caselaw_access_project_filtered}. It contains 6,735,525 U.S. case documents spanning roughly 365 years. This corpus does not provide direct COLIEE supervision; instead, it is used to make ModernBERT better match legal text distributions before contrastive fine-tuning. Under our current hardware budget, masked language modeling on this corpus runs for about 60 hours but still covers only 0.384 epoch, so we treat it as a useful but still under-trained domain adaptation stage.
